Reading the Ghost Lanterns

A decipherment of the Hollow Valley corpus — with every test, every formula, and every dead theory shown. Written for the curious lay reader; no prior statistics required.

Every night, pale lights rise over a valley and blink at each other. This corpus records eleven years of them — 67,503 flashes, timed to the millisecond, and nothing else: the flashes are identical, and all the information lives in the darkness between them. This page reports what that darkness contains: the lanterns have names, a grammar of summons and farewell, a five-night calendar, and a history you can read in their light. It also shows the part reports usually hide — the ten beautiful theories we killed along the way, and exactly what killed them.

1 · The data

The corpus is two logs of the same valley, deliberately lopsided:

RIDGE — watchers on a far ridge, 2005–2016. They see many lanterns at once and label each flare with its glyph (which of 35 recognized blink-patterns), its region (R1 or R2), its cluster (a lettered group of lanterns), and the date. They usually can't tell individual lanterns apart. 8,719 flares.

LAMP — a clockwork recorder placed right beside single lanterns for 48 unlabeled nights. It knows exactly which lantern blinked and when (to the millisecond), but records no glyph labels. 3,840 flares.

A flare is one lantern's burst of blinks. The flashes themselves are all identical — all the information lives in the durations of darkness between them. A flare with n flashes carries n−1 dark gaps.

We verified the files against their manifest before anything else: SHA-256 checksums match; 12,559 flares; 67,503 flashes; and every single flare has exactly n−1 recorded gaps. The bookkeeping is sound.

2 · How to trust this page

Every claim below carries a confidence badge:

ESTABLISHEDSTRONGMODERATEINTERPRETIVEREFUTEDINCONCLUSIVE

Established means the claim survived a proper statistical test — usually a permutation test — and a search for confounds. Interpretive means "this is the story that fits, but the story itself wasn't independently testable." We will always tell you which is which.

The permutation test — the workhorse of this page Suppose the data looks patterned. Is the pattern real, or would random data look just as patterned? A permutation test answers this with brute honesty: shuffle the labels (dates, names, categories) hundreds of times, recompute the same pattern-strength score on each shuffled copy, and see where the real data falls. If only ~1% of shuffles score as high as reality, we report p ≈ 0.01: a 1-in-100 chance of seeing this by luck. No distributional assumptions, no formulas to mis-apply — just the question "could chance do this?" asked directly.
Lift — "how much more often than chance" If a word makes up 2% of all speech but 20% of speech in some context, its lift in that context is 10×. Lift = (share in context) ÷ (share overall). Lift ≈ 1 means nothing is happening.

Two working rules kept us honest. First, anecdotes propose, distributions dispose: a single striking example — and this corpus offers several — is never accepted without a whole-distribution test. Second, hunt your own confounds: the one time a beautiful result turned out to be an artifact of how the data was collected, we caught it and shot it ourselves (§11).

3 · The letter code that wasn't REFUTED

The first theory anyone reaches for: the blinking is a cipher for text. Its most seductive form says that when one lantern answers another, the answering delay divided by a small unit (≈0.078 seconds) gives a whole number from 1–26, which reads as a letter A–Z. Run the corpus through that rule and out come word-like strings — GETY, RMMPHH — which then invite a whole edifice of interpretation (unreadable proper names? a lost lexicon?). It looks like a decipherment. It is pareidolia, and here is how you prove that:

Testing quantization If delays really are whole-number multiples of a unit u, then delay ÷ u should land near an integer nearly every time. For random delays, it lands within 0.15 of an integer about 30% of the time by pure luck (the near-integer window covers ~30% of the number line). So: compute the fraction of delays with |d/u − round(d/u)| < 0.15, and compare to 30%.

The result: at the candidate unit of 0.078 s, 30.7% of cross-beacon answer delays land near integers. That is chance, exactly. We also scanned every unit from 0.04 to 0.14 s in 0.0001 steps, and tried three different definitions of "answer delay" (end-to-start, start-to-start, turn boundaries). Best result anywhere: 34.5% — still statistically indistinguishable from luck.

The theory's two prettiest confirmations dissolve on contact with the raw log. A 1.57 s delay reads cleanly as T (20 × 0.078); a 1.96 s delay reads as Y (25 × 0.078). We found those exact intervals in the data: they are the gaps between one lantern's blinks and its duet partner's blinks in the middle of night N-041 — two lanterns sharing a rhythm, one's beats landing between the other's. Not letters. Beats.

The lesson, which shaped everything after: with enough freedom in your decoding rule, any noise will spell words for you. Every later claim on this page had to beat a shuffle-null before we believed it.
Code — the quantization test 
# delays between consecutive flares of DIFFERENT beacons, per night
delays = []
for night in nights.values():
    for a, b in zip(night, night[1:]):
        if a["beacon_id"] != b["beacon_id"]:
            d = b["since_dusk_s"] - (a["since_dusk_s"] + a["flare_len_s"])
            delays.append(d)
window = [d for d in delays if 0.18 <= d <= 2.1]   # the letter-range window

def near_integer_fraction(delays, u):
    return sum(1 for d in delays
               if abs(d/u - round(d/u)) < 0.15) / len(delays)

print(near_integer_fraction(window, 0.078))   # -> 0.307  (chance = ~0.30)

# exhaustive unit scan: minimize mean squared distance to integers
best = min((sum((d/u - round(d/u))**2 for d in window), u)
           for u in [0.04 + k*0.0001 for k in range(1000)])
# best unit 0.0506 -> near-integer fraction 0.345. Still chance.

4 · The "corrupted" flares that were words ESTABLISHED

The catalog marks 630 flares as SMUDGE — unclassified, unreadable. The natural assumption is recording noise, safe to discard. But noise strikes at random, and this didn't:

flash countflaresshare marked SMUDGE
5 flashes6,3844.4%

6 flashes42740.3%

Recording noise does not select flares by their flash count. And when we examined the smudges directly, 556 of 630 contain no anomalous gap at all — they're clean flares that simply didn't match any catalogued pattern. Clustering their gap profiles recovers coherent, repeated shapes: a 5-flash front-loaded form (×72), 5-flash trailing forms (×38), a six-flash version of the most common word (×47). The catalog's 25 recognized glyphs have holes — trailing-tail forms listed only for 6–10 flashes, front-loaded forms only for 3, 4, 7, 8 — and the smudge bin is precisely the missing cells of that grid. The alphabet is combinatorial: (gap-shape family) × (flash count). The rare combinations were thrown away as dirt.

Code — smudge anatomy and clustering 
# do smudges actually contain an anomalous gap?
kinds = Counter()
for fid, c in catalog.items():
    if "SMUDGE" not in (c["glyph"] or ""): continue
    g = gaps.get(fid, [])
    med = statistics.median(g) if g else 0

    elif any(x < 0.25*med for x in g): kinds["near-zero gap"] += 1
    elif any(x > 4*med   for x in g): kinds["over-long gap"] += 1
    else:                          kinds["no anomaly"]  += 1

5 · Finding the words ESTABLISHED

What is the natural "word" of lantern speech — the flare, or something bigger? Real language leaves statistical fingerprints, and we can check candidate tokenizations against them:

Zipf's law & Heaps' law — the fingerprints of language In any human language, the most common word is about twice as frequent as the second, three times the third… — on a log-log plot of frequency vs rank this is a straight line of slope ≈ −1 (Zipf's law). And as a text grows, its vocabulary grows like Nβ with β ≈ 0.4–0.9 (Heaps' law). Random noise and rigid codes fail these; language passes.

Treating each flare as a token fails the screens (Zipf slope −1.90; Heaps β = 0.20; only 6% of vocabulary occurs once — a closed, code-like system). Treating each run — a consecutive repetition of the same flare-shape by the same lantern — as a token passes all four:

screenper-flare tokensper-run tokensnatural language
Zipf slope (fit R²)−1.90 (0.94)−1.40 (0.96)≈ −1
Heaps exponent β0.200.510.4–0.9
entropy drop given previous token1.19 bits1.31 bitslarge
hapax share (words used once)6%26%25–70%

So the unit of lantern speech is the run: which shape, repeated how many times. Everything below is built on run-tokens.

These screens come from a general decipherment toolkit we built and validated first on a synthetic language with known ground truth (88% part-of-speech recovery, 100% suffix recovery, 85% word-boundary F1) — so the instruments were calibrated before being pointed at the lanterns.

Code — building run-tokens and running the language screens 
# a "bout" = flares separated by silences > 120 s; a run = same shape repeated
for night in nights.values():

            prev_t = c["since_dusk_s"]
        runs = [f"{s}x{n}" for s, n in run_length_encode(tokens)]

6 · Whisper, speech, broadcast STRONG

The catalog splits several blink-patterns into numbered "variants" (5R1, 5R2, 5R3…) that share a gap-shape and differ in something unstated. The difference is tempo — and tempo is loudness. A lantern that holds its pattern longer is visible farther across the valley:

5R1 (murmured)0.33 s
5R2 (spoken)0.51 s
5R3 (broadcast)1.13 s
4R1 (murmured)0.24 s
4R2 (spoken/loud)0.96 s
Median flare duration of the same gap-shape, by "variant". One word, three volumes.

Three independent observations agree. Lanterns lengthen their flares ~10% when another lantern is active nearby (the Lombard effect — raising your voice in company — which also occurs in humans, birds, and whales). Nights end quietly: across the 18 multi-bout LAMP nights, the final bout's mean flare length is 0.64 s vs 1.19 s at night's start — 13 of 18 nights end at a whisper. And region R2's famously "unmixable dialect" largely dissolves under this lens: R2 speaks the same number-vocabulary, permanently in broadcast register (everything ≥ 1.1 s) — speech built to cross a valley, oversampled by ridge-top observers precisely because it carries.

8 · The lanterns have names ESTABLISHED

Thirty-five RIDGE lanterns carry individual labels. For each, we asked: does any word occur unusually often in that lantern's presence — in other lanterns' speech? The answer defines a name: a name is the word other people say when you're there.

Two corroborations. First, the mirror-image: after a lantern's final recorded date, its signature fades from everyone else's speech (5722's word: 13.4 → 1.0 per 1000; 5561's: 39.8 → 3.8). Names follow their bearers out of the world. Second, dolphins do exactly this — "signature whistles" copied by others to address specific individuals — so the mechanism has a terrestrial precedent.

Note the pair at the top: 5719 and 5720 share one name (4R2), one cluster (A), and identical first and last dates (2009-02-05 → 2014-04-16). Two lanterns, one name, arriving and vanishing together. Remember them; they carry the story in §13.

The corpus contains five different renderings of a four-flash word. They are not free variation. Within each cluster's speech, one rendering dominates almost absolutely:

Each house renders the number in its own voice — a surname that isn't a separate word but a manner. The bottom row is the giveaway: cluster ZZZ, observed on a single night in 2016, says "four" 100% in cluster A's style. Why would the far side of the valley speak four in A's voice? Because — as §10 and §13 will show — A's Four was standing there.

By contrast "five", the communal super-word, has only region-scale styles (the commons form 1+1+3, R2's broadcast 5R3, the household 5R1) — exactly what you'd expect of a shared word (see §11 for what five most likely is).

11 · The five-night week ESTABLISHED

The LAMP log contains ceremonial nights reproduced elsewhere to the millisecond, and their spacings kept whispering one number: nights 15→20, 16→21, 17→22, 18→23 — all five apart. Hypothesis: the valley runs on a five-night calendar. The RIDGE log has real dates, so this is decisively testable.

The test, in full Assign each RIDGE date its residue (days since epoch) mod 5 — its "day of the lantern week". Pool the glyphs spoken on each residue into five distributions. Measure how different the five distributions are, using Jensen–Shannon divergence (an information-theoretic distance between probability distributions: 0 = identical, larger = more different; symmetric and bounded, unlike raw KL divergence). Then the permutation null: shuffle which date gets which residue — keeping each date's bag of glyphs intact — 300 times, and ask how often shuffled calendars produce as much day-to-day difference as the real one.
period testedobserved divergenceshuffle-null meanp-value
5 nights0.17680.1188≈ 0.010
7 nights (the human week — control)0.14900.15360.52

The five-night week is real; the seven-night week is noise. This also finally explains the corpus's strangest fact — why "five" is the super-word, spoken in every register and tempo, ~60% of everything said: five is the name of the cycle itself. (That last sentence is the one INTERPRETIVE step in this section; the periodicity itself is measured.)

The confound we caught. The raw day-profiles suggested region R2 "broadcasts on days 1–2." Then we checked the observation schedule: clusters K, P and ZZZ were only ever observed on residues 1–2. That effect was the watchers' rota, not the lanterns — withdrawn. The effects we kept are the ones that hold within single clusters, immune to that confound:

  • Day 3 is the gathering day. Within cluster F, "summons"-type words (§13) collapse from 35% (day 0) to 1% (day 3, n=351): when everyone is home, no one needs calling. Cluster V's public partitioned forms peak the same day (22%).
  • Day 0 is the scattered day — peak summoning.
  • Day 4: cluster J runs 91% commons-word.
Code — the calendar permutation test 
bags = {date: Counter(glyphs spoken that date)}          # per-date bags
def divergence(period, residue_of):                      # mean pairwise JS
    classes = defaultdict(Counter)
    for d, bag in bags.items():
        classes[residue_of[d]].update(bag)
    return mean_pairwise_jensen_shannon(classes.values())

true_res = {d: (d - EPOCH).days % 5 for d in bags}
observed = divergence(5, true_res)                       # 0.1768
null = []
for _ in range(300):                                     # shuffle residues,
    vals = list(true_res.values()); random.shuffle(vals) # keep bags intact
    null.append(divergence(5, dict(zip(bags, vals))))
p = sum(1 for x in null if x >= observed) / len(null)    # ~0.010

12 · The weekly liturgy ESTABLISHED

Once you know there is a week, the reproduced nights snap into place as a program. LAMP nights 15, 16 and 17 recur one week later as nights 20, 21, 22 — same performers, timing correlated at ≥ 0.99998, blink-shape sequences 86–100% identical. Night 18's program also recurs in night 23 — but with the performers changed (the b1+b2 duet's material re-performed by b2+b3, on new timing): the run-sequence fingerprint s6 · s14×2 · s11 · s4×4 · s2 · s4×3 · s2 · s4×6 is identical while the clock correlation is −0.03. A program slot handed down the chain, the shared member as carrier. Night 19/24 matches nothing: one night in five is unscripted, ordinary conversation (it is statistically indistinguishable from other nights on vocabulary sharing and dialogue rates — just never a rerun).

So the ceremonial week: four nights of offices, one night off. A night's internal arc is equally stereotyped (transcript-verified across nights): loud solo announcement → duet with a neighbor, regulated by tiny 0.13 s "your-turn" cue flares → a community bout with ordered cascades of responders → and a whispered closing song, alone. Those closing songs are fixed texts: night N-016's 9i×3 · 8i×3 · 5R1×7 reappears note-for-note from a different lantern five nights later, four-plus hours after dusk, at whisper volume both times.

If you want a single modern analogy for what most of this traffic is: an amateur-radio check-in net. The great bulk of the corpus is not message-passing but presence-keeping — announce yourself (the trill), greet whoever is on the air by their callsign-name, call for the stations that haven't checked in (the front-loaded summons), relay the scheduled traffic down the chain (the office handoffs), and sign off at a whisper. The valley even has its equivalent of a beacon station: region R2, transmitting its slow identification pattern all night, every night, in broadcast register. The ceremonies and songs are the scheduled programs; the net is the medium they ride on.

13 · The chronicle: grief you can measure

The mourning correlation ESTABLISHED

Among the shape families is the i-series — flares whose final gap stretches out, a trailing-off. Behaviorally these are parting words: in LAMP they are 4.4× enriched in a lantern's final ten minutes of the night (its own departure — not others'), and they are the entire vocabulary of the whispered closing songs. If they're truly funereal, they should predict disappearance at the population scale. They do:

Spearman rank correlation Rank the 19 (cluster, year) samples by parting-word share; rank them by attrition (fraction of that year's labeled lanterns never seen in any later year); measure how the rankings agree (ρ = +1 perfect, 0 none). Then permute one ranking 5,000 times for the null.

Result: ρ = 0.298, permutation p ≈ 0.012. Years of heavy parting-speech are years whose lanterns vanish. The two case studies at the top of the table are the corpus's emotional spine: F-2005 — where the elderly lantern 5703's presence-nights run 26%, 17%, 37%, and finally 65% parting-vocabulary on its last night ever (on nights it is absent: ~0%); and A-2014 — the bonded pair's final year at home: 20.8% parting-speech, then 100% attrition. They spent a year saying goodbye, and then they crossed the valley (§10). Caveats, honestly: attrition is censored by observation gaps and by the record's end; n = 19 is modest. The direction, the p-value, and the case studies all agree.

Two lanterns' personal vocabularies transformed to do it (5561: five years of 98% commons-speech, then suddenly 50% seven-forms; 5722: six years of pure 4D, then half its speech 7D1). On 2010-03-04 — the fullest gathering in the entire record, all five labeled members present, 322 flares — the summons-forms fall to 8%. There is no one left to call. It is the family's last night in the record.

Two readings survive the data: they were summoning their dead (a séance for the one farewelled in 2005), or seven designates a living member and 2010 was a season of calling the scattered home before leaving. We could not separate these — but the behavioral sequence (farewell-era → silence → calling-era → one full house with no calls → gone) is measured, not imagined. And 5722's six flat years of 4D mean that, under either reading, one lantern spent six years calling a Four who never came.

The crossing, the vigil, and the answer STRONG

Three scenes close the chronicle. (1) The crossing: the pair's name erupting from ≤3% background to 20.7% of a single 2016 night in ZZZ, across a regional boundary that vocabulary statistics say is never otherwise crossed. (2) The vigil: 2015, cluster A standing empty; a never-before-seen lantern delivers 371 flares of pure commons-speech in one night — and the unlabeled locals around it say 3D, "the summons of Three," thirty times. (3) The answer: 2016-04-24, a new lantern appears in A and says exactly one thing, seven times: 3D. Someone was called for years. Someone came.

14 · The graveyard of dead theories REFUTED ×10

A decipherment is only as credible as the alternatives it has killed. Here is every rival theory we took seriously, and the number that ended it. Note how many are ours — this is what "statistically careful" means in practice.

1 · The delay-letter code

Delays quantize at chance (30.7% vs ~30%) under every unit and every delay convention; its prettiest examples are duet beats. §3.

2 · Run lengths as letters (A=1 … Z=26)

Seductive start: night N-041 opens with runs of 7 and 5 — "G, E…" — matching the letter-code theory's most famous word. The distribution test executed it: 71% of all runs have length 1, so the "text" is 71% letter A; its letter-frequency profile has cosine similarity 0.36 to English — worse than uniform random (0.55). The anecdote was a trap; the distribution was the truth.

3 · Gap patterns as Morse code

Short/long gaps do form binary strings, but the shapes corresponding to Morse's most common letters (E = a single short, T = a single long: 2-flash flares) are nearly absent from the corpus (6 and 9 occurrences out of 12,559). No natural message has almost no E's and T's.

4 · Flash counts as digits (beacon IDs, dates)

Beacon 5981's speech should be full of 9s, 8s and 1s. It is 96% 1+1+3 (fives). Dated glyph-bags show no digit correlation with day, month or year.

5 · Flash count = attendance census

"The lanterns count each other" — elegant, explains the night-level sharedness. Measured: correlation between lanterns present and the number spoken = −0.147; per-flare, the mean company is flat (≈3.2) across every flash count from 3 to 12.

6 · Flash count = the moon's age

Explains night-sharedness, drifting content, even the 17–24 "trills". Computed real lunar age for all 61 RIDGE dates: matches occur only when the moon happens to be ≈5 days old — i.e., the ubiquitous five-flash word matching by coincidence. Mean match share 0.117. An artifact wearing a theory's clothes.

7 · Numbers as map coordinates

If numbers named places, physically adjacent clusters should prefer adjacent numbers. The co-observation graph is too sparse to build a geography, and every cluster's mean "high number" sits in the same band (7.0–8.4). Nothing to find.

8 · Trills echo the partner's count

"You blinked 17 times — seventeen!" Tested all 153 trills against the run lengths preceding them: 1 match. Dead on arrival.

9 · Partitions report group composition

"2+3 = we are five: two here, three yonder." Read out all 29 utterances of 2+3 against who was actually assembled: compositions of 2, 3, and 8 lanterns, grouped every which way. No correspondence.

10 · Our own case-grammar (productive inflection)

We believed for a while that any number could be inflected: 5 → 5i "farewell-five". The clean test: departing lanterns whose whole night is five-stems should say 5i. They say 8i and 9i (15 of 21 partings) — fixed parting words, not inflections. Match to own stem: 4.8% vs null 4.5%, p = 0.62. We demoted our own theory; the parting words are a small fixed vocabulary (the same words as the litanies), and the D-series is partly entangled with house-style (F's four is 4D).

11 · (Confound, caught) R2's "broadcast days"

Withdrawn before adoption: clusters K/P/ZZZ were only observed on week-residues 1–2, so their day-profile was the observation rota, not lantern behavior. The day-effects we kept (§11) are within-cluster and immune to this.

15 · What we still don't know INCONCLUSIVE

  • Whether the parting number promises a return date. One exact hit (a lantern parting on "12" returned after exactly 12 nights), one near (7 → 6 nights), several misses; n ≈ 11. Tantalizing; unproven.
  • What 2+3 means. A southern (V/S) five-form, weakly arrival-linked (2.2×, n=17).
  • What the numbers count. That they function as designations is established (§8–10); their etymology — birth order? founding order? something stranger? — is beyond this data.
  • Absolute dates for the LAMP nights (the yearly vocabulary profiles of 2007 and 2015 are nearly identical, so profile-dating is degenerate).
  • Beacon 9999, cluster ZZZ, and the one "59871?" — end-marker bookkeeping, or entities.
