what is happening here

A midi file only records which piano key was pressed — and 12-tet is a lossy projection. It tempers out a kernel of commas (81/80, 128/125, …), collapsing intervals that other tunings keep distinct: the same four keys could mean a 5/4 third, an 81/64 third, or a 9/7. So "convert this melody to 19-edo" is underdetermined until you pick an interpretation. This page offers three, and an auto mode that scores them:

direct — each note goes to the nearest step of the target: round(semitones · edo/12). Preserves the literal cents of the melody; ignores its grammar. The honest choice for atonal material.

fifths — the regular-temperament mapping. Every 12-tet note is respelled on the chain of fifths (key-aware: the detected key decides F♯ vs G♭), then fifth and octave are sent to the target's best fifth and octave — a homomorphism on the ⟨2,3⟩ lattice. A major third stops being "4 keys" and becomes what it structurally is: four fifths up, two octaves down. In 19 or 31-edo that lands meantone-flat and sweet; in 22-edo it lands superpyth-sharp. Function is preserved; color follows the tuning's own logic.

just — the harmonic interpretation. Each pitch class is read as a 5-limit ratio against the detected tonic (5/4, 3/2, 16/15…) and the target step nearest that ratio is chosen. Anchoring to the tonic rather than chaining local intervals is deliberate: chained-JI drifts by a comma around progressions like I–vi–ii–V (the comma pump), and a fixed anchor absorbs that instead of accumulating it.

auto detects the key (Krumhansl–Schmuckler profile correlation, duration-weighted). Low confidence ⇒ direct. Otherwise it computes, for every note, how far each candidate mapping lands from the just intervals the tonal music implies, and picks the smallest — with a small structural bonus for fifths, which stays coherent under transposition and modulation.

Timbre: consonance is not in the intervals alone — it is the interaction of the tuning with the spectrum (Sethares). The instrument here is a partial-generator, and matched snaps every partial to a power of the target's step, so the beating that makes "out of tune" sound out of tune lines up with the tuning itself. Auto keeps harmonic partials when the edo approximates harmonics 3·5·7 well, and matches them when it doesn't (13, 16…) — that is what makes a 13-edo triad genuinely consonant. The original button always plays literal 12-tet with harmonic partials, for honest A/B.

What cannot survive: enharmonic puns. A diminished seventh that resolves two ways, an augmented sixth reread as a dominant seventh — those are uses of 12's kernel, and a tuning that doesn't temper the same commas must choose one reading. The fifth-chain spelling makes that choice for you (per key), which is the best any translation can do.
